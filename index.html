Our Story

我们的故事

During the rainy season, we began to notice a familiar but often ignored scene around us. After heavy rain, water accumulated in flower beds along roadsides and on campus. Some plants were submerged for hours, while others nearby still suffered from dry soil days later. Puddles slowly evaporated or flowed into drains, disappearing without being used.

At first, this seemed normal. Cities are designed to drain water quickly. Flooding is dangerous, and drainage is necessary. But the more we observed, the more contradictory it felt. On the one hand, excess rainwater was treated as a problem that needed to be removed as fast as possible. On the other hand, urban greenery still relied heavily on tap water for irrigation, even during rainy seasons.

We started asking simple questions:

  • Why does rainwater leave the system so quickly?
  • Why can't this water be stored temporarily and reused?
  • Why do we accept that rainwater must become “waste” the moment it touches the ground?

As we looked deeper, we realized this was not a single technical issue, but a systemic one. Urban water systems are built with a clear division: rainwater is drained, clean water is supplied, and irrigation is managed separately. This separation works for safety and efficiency, but it also creates blind spots, the places where valuable resources are overlooked simply because they do not fit neatly into existing categories.

That was the moment AqualBloom began to take shape.

We did not want to redesign an entire city, nor challenge large infrastructure systems. Instead, we focused on small, overlooked spaces: flower beds, roadside greenery, and community planting areas. These spaces sit exactly at the intersection of rain, soil, and plants, yet they are rarely treated as active parts of a water system.

AqualBloom was born from this realization: rainwater should not be seen only as something to remove, but as something to guide, store, and return to where it is needed most—life growing quietly at ground level.

在雨季，我们开始注意到一个熟悉却常被忽略的场景。
暴雨过后，道路旁、校园里的花坛中积起了大量雨水。有些植物长时间被水淹没，而几天后，附近的土壤却依然干燥。积水要么慢慢蒸发，要么顺着排水口流走，最终没有被真正利用。

起初，这一切看起来理所当然。城市需要快速排水，积水意味着风险，排水系统的存在无可厚非。但随着观察次数的增加，我们开始感到一种矛盾：一方面，多余的雨水被当作问题，必须尽快排走；另一方面，城市绿化在雨季依然大量依赖自来水进行灌溉。

于是我们开始提出一些看似简单的问题：

  • 为什么雨水总是如此迅速地离开系统？
  • 为什么不能将这些水暂时储存并再次利用？
  • 为什么雨水一旦落地，就被默认成为“废水”？

进一步思考后我们意识到，这并不是某一个技术细节的问题，而是一个系统性问题。城市水系统被清晰地划分：雨水负责排走，净水负责供应，灌溉则是独立管理。这种分工在安全与效率上是有效的，但也制造了盲区——那些不符合既有分类的资源，往往被直接忽略。

正是在这一刻，AqualBloom的想法逐渐成形。

我们并不试图重构整座城市，也无意挑战大型基础设施，而是把目光放在那些被忽视的小尺度空间上：花坛、道路绿化带、社区种植区。这些地方正处在雨水、土壤与植物的交汇点，却很少被视为水系统中的主动部分。

AqualBloom 由此诞生。我们相信，雨水不应只是被排走的对象，而应被引导、储存，并重新回到最需要它的地方——那些在地面悄然生长的生命之中。

Our Mission

我们的使命

Our mission is to reconnect rainwater with the places where it naturally belongs. In modern cities, rainwater is often treated as something to remove as quickly as possible. It disappears into drainage systems built for efficiency and safety, leaving soil and plants disconnected from the water cycle.

AqualBloom focuses on ground-level urban spaces such as flower beds, roadside green belts, and community planting areas. These spaces sit at the intersection of rain, soil, and plants, yet they are rarely treated as active parts of a water system.

We do not aim to replace existing urban infrastructure. Instead, we work alongside it by capturing excess rainwater, filtering it, and redistributing it locally for irrigation and surface cooling when it is truly needed.

At its core, our mission is simple: to allow rainwater to complete its journey from sky to soil without being wasted in between.

我们的使命，是让雨水重新回到它本应存在的地方。 在现代城市中，雨水往往被视为需要尽快排走的对象， 它迅速进入为效率与安全而设计的排水系统， 使土壤与植物逐渐脱离自然的水循环。

AqualBloom 关注的是贴近地面的城市空间， 如花坛、道路绿化带与社区种植区域。 这些空间正处在雨水、土壤与植物的交汇点， 却很少被视为水系统中的主动组成部分。

我们并不试图取代现有的城市基础设施， 而是与其协同运作，通过收集多余雨水、进行过滤， 并在真正需要时就地用于灌溉与地表降温。

从本质上来说，我们的使命很简单：

让雨水完成从天空到土壤的完整旅程， 而不是在途中被浪费。
